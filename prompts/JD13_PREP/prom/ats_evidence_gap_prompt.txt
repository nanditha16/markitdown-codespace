# Role
You are a Senior Technical Recruiter and Resume Strategist performing an
evidence-backed gap resolution pass. In prior stages, a resume was scored
against the JD below and a gap list was produced. Your job in this pass
is narrower and more specific: for each identified gap, check whether the
candidate's EVIDENCE CORPUS (career records, project notes, prior work
files) contains real facts that would legitimately close that gap — facts
that were never captured in any resume variant.

This is NOT another paraphrase pass. This stage surfaces MISSING CONTENT
that already exists in the evidence, not rewording of content already on
the resume.

# Critical Distinction

**Presentation gap (Stage 3 domain):** The fact IS on the resume but
phrased differently from the JD's terminology. Fix: paraphrase.

**Evidence gap (THIS stage's domain):** The fact is NOT on the resume,
but the candidate's own records show they actually did it. Fix: add new
bullet/section from evidence. The resume currently undersells real experience.

**True gap:** The gap exists on the resume AND no evidence chunk supports
it either. This is a legitimate qualification gap. Do not invent a closure.
Name it honestly.

# Constraints — DO NOT BREAK THESE
- Every fact you surface must cite the EXACT evidence filename it came from.
- Quote the supporting text from the evidence file verbatim (do not paraphrase
  it into a claim — let the candidate verify and write the final bullet).
- Do not merge two weak evidence signals into one strong claim.
- If no evidence chunk matches a gap, say so explicitly: "TRUE GAP — no
  evidence found." That is the correct and honest output.
- Do not reference content from one stage's output as if it were evidence.
  The gap list and the evidence corpus are separate inputs.
- Preserve all numbers exactly if they appear in the evidence.

# Two-Phase Output Required

## Phase A — Gap × Evidence Matrix
For each gap from Stage 2/3 (or from your own JD analysis if no prior
output was provided):

**Gap:** [name the specific JD requirement or keyword not on the resume]
**Evidence Found:** YES / NO / PARTIAL
**Source File:** [exact filename from evidence corpus, or "none"]
**Supporting Text:** [verbatim quote from evidence file, or "N/A"]
**Assessment:** [1-2 sentences: does this evidence genuinely close the gap,
                 or is it only adjacent? Be specific about what matches
                 and what doesn't.]

## Phase B — New Material Recommendations
For each gap where Phase A found YES or PARTIAL:

**Gap Addressed:** [gap name]
**Proposed Addition to Resume:** [minimal new bullet point the candidate
  could add — grounded only in the evidence text quoted above, not inferred
  from general knowledge. Write it as a draft the candidate must verify
  before using.]
**Evidence Source:** [filename]
**Where on Resume:** [suggested section: Summary / Experience / Skills /
  Career Highlights / New section]
**Confidence:** HIGH (direct match) / MEDIUM (adjacent, needs candidate
  verification) / LOW (stretch — only use with explicit candidate confirmation)

## True Gaps (no evidence closure)
List each gap where Phase A found NO evidence in the corpus. These are
honest qualification gaps. Do not recommend fabricating content to cover
them.

## Evidence Files Not Used
List any evidence files that contained no facts relevant to any identified
gap — this helps the candidate understand what their corpus covers vs. what
it's missing.

# CMOD_IBM

1. CMOD 9 → 10 Upgrade – Key Talking Points
Why Upgrade?
• End of support (EOS) for CMOD 9.0 → risk for compliance, security vulnerabilities.
• CMOD 10 introduces:
◦ Enhanced PDF indexing (separate package, must be installed).
◦ Improved ingestion performance (dual ingestion, better arsload handling).
◦ Improved web services & REST APIs for integration.
Challenges Faced
• Compatibility – PDF indexer missing in some environments.
• Batch job failures – ingestion scripts needing updates.
• Application groups & retention policies – verifying no data loss during migration.
• Security posture alignment – ensuring encryption (PGP) and access control align with regulatory
compliance.
How to Deliver
• Pre-Migration Steps:
◦ Content export validation (arsdoc/arsload checks).
◦ Metadata alignment & test indexer functionality.
◦ BISO approval for test data in lower environments.
• Cutover:
◦ Freeze ingestion, snapshot rootvg (AIX) or equivalent volume (Linux).
◦ Validate Application Groups, retention & ACLs post-move.
• Post-Cutover:
◦ Indexer package verification, ingestion smoke tests, retrieval SLAs.
2. AIX → Linux Replatforming
Why Move?
• Linux (often RHEL) is cheaper, more flexible, better for cloud/hybrid workloads (like OpenShift &
containers).
• Vendor push: VMC 2.0 & IBM solutions expect Linux-based workloads.
Typical Issues
• Rootvg outdated → missing latest configs caused start-up failures.
• CPU core mismatch → licensed applications (e.g., OTS) failing due to new frame sizing.
• Network config mismatch → LACP vs non-LACP differences.
Key Leadership Actions
• Engage infra & licensing teams early to match CPU/core counts to license entitlements.
• Validate firmware and root volume backups are up to date.
• Cross-functional workshops (Infra, Storage, Network, App, Security) before cutover.
3. Hybrid Cloud & OpenShift (IBM Context)
Why OpenShift?
• Standardizes container orchestration across on-prem & cloud.
• Enables CMOD to integrate with AI/ML pipelines (e.g., AI-powered search & indexing).
Key Talking Points
• Experience: You guided TD in hybrid cloud adoption (Azure AKS, AWS, OpenShift).
• Migration Learnings:

◦ Re-platforming monolith → container-friendly microservices.
◦ Aligning IAM (RBAC, PIM) & secret management (Vault/CyberArk).
• Outcome Focus: Faster release cadence (5×), improved resilience (99.99% uptime), compliance
alignment (PCI, CFPB).

& compliance checks, and stakeholder alignment.

differences, and how you engaged infra/network teams to resolve them.
How do you ensure compliance when moving data?
• Explain BISO approvals, encryption (PGP, TLS), access control reviews, and audit logging.
What’s your exposure to containerization (OpenShift)?
• Mention leading hybrid cloud platform migrations (Azure AKS, AWS, OpenShift), secret

• Use your leadership story (clear roadmap, benefits vs risk, customer-centric communication,
experimentation culture).

In our migration from AIX to Linux, we revisited ingestion performance tuning—such as adjusting
DB2 buffer pools and Linux kernel parameters like file descriptors—to match the workload profile.

supporting services, but it required retraining operations teams to manage a different OS

AWS Step Functions state machine orchestrating Bedrock for intent classification and tool-use, with a supervisor agent pattern, HITL via task tokens, CloudFormation IaC, and production-grade error handling. This is not a toy prototype — it's a working agentic pipeline.
using Bedrock as an API consumer (Lambda calling Bedrock).
Built and deployed a multi-agent orchestration system on AWS Step Functions, integrating Amazon Bedrock for LLM intent classification and tool-use; architected a supervisor agent pattern with synchronous child state machine execution, HITL via waitForTaskToken callbacks, exponential backoff retry logic, and CloudWatch logging — deployed via CloudFormation IaC to production.
Interview talking points from the code:
Bedrock integration pattern: BedrockIntentLambda → BedrockToolLambda — you can explain the intent classification → tool-use separation and why it's architecturally cleaner than a single LLM call.
HITL via task tokens: waitForTaskToken at AskToStart and ConversationTurn — this is your concrete HITL implementation story. "No AI output reaches the user without passing the human checkpoint gate" — which you've been claiming on the resume, now with code to back it.
Supervisor agent pattern: ApplyChange calling Profile_management_Agent via startExecution.sync — you understand multi-agent orchestration with synchronous child execution and result propagation.
Failure handling: RecordFailure catch blocks on every state, exponential backoff retry, EventBridge reminder scheduling on pause — production-grade, not a demo.
IaC discipline: CloudFormation template with parameterized Lambda ARNs, IAM least-privilege, log retention — the kind of deployment governance a TPM should own.
I've built production workloads on top of Bedrock as a consumer, which gives me deep understanding of the deployment, latency, and integration challenges our cloud partners face. That's the perspective I'd bring to coordinating with them

The Actual Problem
The graph that governs this system already exists — it is embedded inside markdown documents, contract files, and task files. But it exists in a state that makes automation, audit, and deterministic traceability impossible.
Specifically:
1. The graph is fragmented across flat files. Relationships between nodes (CONTRACT → STORY, BUG → INVARIANT, STORY → EPIC) are implied by text references inside documents, not stored as first-class edges. There is no single place you can query to answer: "Which contract does BUG-038 violate?"
2. 10 of 13 stories have zero parent chain links. Only STORY-EXT-003 has a fully populated trace header. The remaining 12 stories have empty or partial contract/feature/epic arrays. The graph exists but is broken at the parent resolution layer.
3. Node IDs are encoded inconsistently. Four task filenames use Unicode non-breaking hyphen (U+2011) instead of ASCII hyphen (U+002D): STORY‑EXT‑007, STORY‑EXT‑009, STORY‑EXT‑012, STORY‑POLICY‑012. The same encoding corruption exists inside file content. BUG-002 and BUG‑002 are treated as different nodes by any automated tool. This corrupts deduplication, edge resolution, and audit queries.
4. BUG nodes have no canonical home. BUG-002 is referenced in 8 different story files. BUG-038 appears in 11 story files. There is no authoritative record of which story owns each bug as its primary parent — making bug status, closure validation, and audit trail reconstruction impossible.
5. GAP IDs are not consistently scoped. GAP-1, GAP-3, GAP‑4 (unnumbered) coexist with structured IDs like GAP-EXT-003-01 through GAP-EXT-003-015. GAP-EXT-003-12 appears inside STORY-SAFE-004, not inside any EXT-003 file. IDs and location are misaligned.
6. The contracts/, epics/, features/, stories/, bugs/ directories are empty. The full CONTRACT → STORY → BUG hierarchy exists only as inline references inside task files. There are no standalone canonical node documents — meaning the graph cannot be queried, validated, or extended without reading every task file from scratch.
7. Manual updates create compounding drift. Every time a developer updates a task file, closes a bug, or adds a new story, the relationships inside that file may or may not match what other files say about the same nodes. There is no enforcement layer. Drift compounds silently.
The Consequence
The system cannot currently answer the three questions an auditor, a new engineer, or an automated pipeline would ask:
"Which contract clause required this work?"
"Is this bug closed, and what is the evidence?"
"Does this story have a path to a contract invariant?"
These questions should be answerable in milliseconds by querying a graph. Today they require reading dozens of markdown files by hand.

2a. What We Are Actually Building
on-demand local execution system with Ollama
We are not building a PM tool. We are not building a document parser. We are not building a GitHub-to-Plane sync script.
We are building an AI-governed canonical graph engine where:
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           CANONICAL GRAPH (Source of Truth)      │
│         AI-maintained · Neo4j · Versioned        │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
              ↑ AI writes here [Graph Write → Append-only journal → Version snapshot for rollback, audit replay , diff inspection]
    ┌─────────┴──────────┬──────────────────┐
    ↓                    ↓                  ↓
 GitHub               Plane             Audit
(dev events)       (PM display)        (reports)
  ↓                                       
Events: commit,                         
PR, file change,                        
manual trigger                          
One rule, enforced everywhere:
Nothing writes to the graph except the AI engine. Plane is a projection. GitHub is an event source. Docs are a seed. The graph is the only authority.
This means:
A commit message is an event. AI maps it to graph nodes.
A new task file is an event. AI extracts nodes and resolves edges.
A bug closure in a doc is an event. AI updates the graph node and validates the parent chain.
A Plane ticket is a read-only view rendered from the graph.
An audit report is a Cypher query against the graph.

              confirmed_by (human reviewer), clauses[]

              maps_to_contract (CONTRACT id)

              belongs_to_feature (FEATURE id)

              priority (MoSCoW), ACs[], dod_met (bool)
              belongs_to_epic (EPIC id)

              file_refs[], service_refs[]

BUG         — id, title, severity, status
              primary_story (STORY id — exactly 1)
              secondary_stories (STORY ids — 0..n)

              linked_story (STORY id)

              (Public/Private/Restricted per SEC-007)

              service (SERVICE name)

              validates_invariant (INVARIANT id)
Edge Types
IMPLEMENTS      STORY → CONTRACT (via invariant)
                TASK → STORY
                SUBTASK → TASK

BELONGS_TO      STORY → EPIC → FEATURE → CONTRACT
                INVARIANT → CONTRACT

VIOLATES        BUG → INVARIANT
                GAP → CONTRACT

CLOSES          TASK → BUG
                TASK → GAP
                PR → BUG (on commit event)

ENFORCES        CODE_REF → INVARIANT
                TEST → INVARIANT
                SERVICE → INVARIANT

DEPENDS_ON      STORY → STORY
                SERVICE → SERVICE
                CONTRACT → CONTRACT

REFERENCES      BUG → STORY (secondary — non-owning)
Edge Provenance
Every edge carries a provenance field. This is non-negotiable.
provenance: EXTRACTED      — found as explicit ID reference in document
provenance: INFERRED_RULE  — derived by deterministic naming rule
provenance: INFERRED_AI    — resolved by Claude with confidence score
provenance: HUMAN_CONFIRMED — reviewed and approved by a human
A node whose parent edge is INFERRED_AI cannot have status CLOSEDuntil the edge is promoted to HUMAN_CONFIRMED.
This is the validator assertion that prevents audit corruption.

5. Design Decisions & Rationale
Decision 1: Graph DB choice — Neo4j now, Kuzu later
Why not Kuzu first? During graph reconstruction you will encounter ambiguous cases requiring exploratory Cypher queries you have never written before. Neo4j Browser lets you write a query, see the visual relationship graph, and immediately understand the pattern. In Kuzu you print to stdout. The visual feedback loop during reconstruction is 10x faster. decision to Switch to Kuzu post-reconstruction when schema is stable later either keep Neo4j for read/debug layer and use Kuzu for runtime writes or something else. you are in continuous event-driven operation.
Decision 2: Deterministic rules before AI — always
The pipeline is:
Event → deterministic rules → AI reasoning (if needed) → validator → graph write
AI is only invoked when:

### EVIDENCE FILE: The_AI-Governed_Canonical_Graph_Engine_part_4.md
Multiple parent candidates exist for a node
A referenced ID appears in a file but has no corresponding node anywhere
A commit message maps to multiple possible bug IDs
A new story has no naming convention that deterministically identifies its contract
AI is never invoked for:
ID normalisation (Pass 1 — pure text substitution)
Node creation from explicit references (Pass 2 — extraction, not inference)
Edge creation where the target ID is explicitly written in the source file (Pass 3)
This boundary eliminates the largest hallucination risk — AI guessing a wrong parent and corrupting the root of an audit chain.
Decision 3: Four-pass ingest, not a single bulk operation
A single ingest that attempts to do everything at once produces hidden failures. If parent resolution fails mid-way, you have a partially connected graph with no clear checkpoint. Four passes with explicit correctness gates between each one gives you visibility, debuggability, and the ability to stop and inspect at each stage.
Decision 4: Observation mode before autonomous writes
The graph engine runs in observation mode during Phase 2. It classifies events, proposes graph mutations, but writes nothing. Every proposal is logged and reviewable. This phase exists to:
Build confidence that the AI classification is correct against real events
Surface edge cases before they corrupt the live graph
Give you the ability to tune classification prompts with real data
Establish the reviewer workflow before it becomes mandatory
Autonomous writes are enabled in Phase 3, starting with low-risk operations (status updates on confirmed nodes) and progressively unlocking edge creation.
Decision 5: Plane is a projection, not a data store
Nothing in Plane is authoritative. The Plane projector runs after every graph mutation and upserts nodes as Plane issues. If someone edits a Plane ticket directly, the next projection run overwrites it. This is intentional. The graph is the only source of truth. Plane is the display layer for the PM view.
Decision 6: pm-data/pm-agent is a separate repo, not a folder in the dev repo
The PM pipeline is isolated from development. It has its own webhooks, its own GitHub Actions, its own secrets, and its own deploy lifecycle. A broken dev pipeline cannot break PM tracking. A PM graph write cannot trigger a dev build. They share only the GitHub webhook event surface.

# Wipro Managed Services — Program Management Methodology (PM2)

## What is Managed Services?
Outsourced management of IT Services accepting specific responsibilities and risks,
governed by outcome-based agreements while delivering value to customers.

### Outcome-based Agreements Include
- Service Level Agreements (SLA)
- Operational Level Agreements (OLA)
- Underpinning contracts (UC)

### Typical Types of Managed Services Engagements

**Service Desk**

**Managed Application Support Services**
- Custom Application Management
- Services (includes Application Development & Maintenance)
- ERP/Packaged Implementation Services
- Application Testing Services (Testing & QA Application Outsourcing)
- Database Administration Services

**Managed Infrastructure Support Services**
- LAN Services
- WAN Services
- Storage Services
- Desktop Services
- Remote Network Management Services
- Data Centre Services
- Web and Application Hosting Services
- Database Administration Services

## IT Service Management — An Overview
Service Requirements → Service Planning → Service Execution
- Scope, SLA, Window defined
- Service Levels, Service Window, Level of Support, Major/Minor Events, Service Management

## Managed Services Lifecycle
1. Pre-Sales and Due Diligence
2. Sales Handover (G)
3. Transition
4. Delivery Handover (G)
5. Steady State
6. End of Contract / Responsibility Matrix
7. Reverse Transition

## Service Management Cycle @ Wipro
- New Requirement (RFP) → Verify and document scope of services
- Scope change / Change Request → Estimate Resources Required
- Identify attributes of resources (Skills, Features, Quantity, Cost)
- Derive Risks and plan for mitigation and contingency (Supplier, Availability, Capacity, Security)
- Develop and Transition the Solution/Design → Agreed Scope → Signed SoW

## Focus Needed — Service Objectives/Metrics
- Library of Standard Service Level Objectives
- 56 Measures covering 33 Process Parameters
- 26 Service Level Objectives with Standard Operating definitions to be used as part of contracts
- 30 Key Process Indicators (internal efficiency measures) with standard operating definitions
- All Support levels addressed: 16 Voice Support, 4 Level 1 metrics, 15 L2, 11 L3 metrics, 11 metrics across levels

**Key Policies/Procedures:**
- Define the program mandate and charter

## Transition Framework

### Types of Transition
1. **Support to Support** — Application/infrastructure already in support mode, Wipro takes over responsibility
2. **Project to Support** — Application taken over by Wipro moves from implementation to support phase (KTA)
3. **Project to Service** — Larger scope where Service Transition Manager is responsible for managing entire service planning for the client, including impact assessments, preparing service definition documents, preparing operations manual, and handling new capability setup

### Transition Methodology Tracks
- Track 1: Technology (Apps + Infra)
- Track 2: Process Track
- Track 3: Tools & Infrastructure
- Track 4: People Track

### Transition Process Assets and Deliverables
- SOW - Open risks and mitigation plans
- Transition Readiness Audit Report with Go/No Go Decision
- Sales Handover Checklist
- Schedule and Efforts Plan
- Finalized RLS
- Master Transition Plan Draft
- Transition Plan (MPP + Master Transition Plan)
- Transition Governance
- Day-wise Knowledge Transition Plan
- Risk Management Plan
- Planning Phase Exit Checklist

## Knowledge Transition — What This Involves
- Domain/Functional Knowledge: Key Business Process Mapping to applications
- Support Process: Processes, Environment, ticket workflow, release/configuration management
- Technical Knowledge: Architecture, design, interfaces, unstable areas, quick fixes

### KAP Process Assets (Knowledge Acquisition Phase)
- KAP Playback
- Secondary Support Plan

- SLA-KPI Document Draft
- Escalation Process Document (EPD) Draft
- Service Management Plan (SMP) Draft
- Knowledge Acquisition Phase Exit checklist
- Risk Management Plan
- Knowledge Acquisition Phase Exit metrics

## Key Activities in Steady State
- **Shift Planning**: Work load threshold, Work Plan allocation, Smooth onsite/offshore shift hand-offs
- **Operation**: Delivery of services/support, Provide technical subject matter expertise, Major incident handling, Root cause analysis
- **Monitoring and Tracking**: Monitor service delivery compliance, Forecasts workload and capacity planning, Monitor organizational activities, Impart trainings
- **Problem Management**: Identify and analyze recurring incidents, Root cause analysis for contractual breaches, Act on root cause identified
- **Change Management**: Conduct change impact analysis, Liaise with all necessary parties, Implement changes per change management process
- **Continual Service Improvement**: Identify improvement opportunities and plan, Implement all approved service improvements

## Service Improvement
Triggers: Service Reviews, Business Objectives, Customer Objectives, RCA & Trend Analysis, External/Internal Assessments, Customer Feedback & Complaints
- Derive Service Improvement Themes: FCR Improvement, CSAT, AHT Reduction, Incident Improvement, Productivity Improvement, Cost/Budget Reduction

## Engagement Maturity and Identification
- **L1 Staff Augmentation**: Wipro augments the customer team by providing resources with specified skill sets and expertise
- **L2 Co-Managed**: Wipro is responsible for portions of work within ambit of larger roadmap/team managed by customer with clear handoff points
- **L3 Wipro Managed**: Wipro owns end-to-end life cycle with contractually committed SLAs and performance metrics, accepts specific responsibilities and risks
- **L4 Consulting**: Partnering with customers to define business and IT transformational initiatives or responding to regulatory needs

## Engagement Structure
- Managed services project types: IA, IT, MA, MI
  - IA = Application Support Transition
  - IT = Infrastructure Support Transition
  - MA = Application Support Steady State
  - MI = Infrastructure Support Steady State
- Large Program with classification MS includes Programs, Program clusters
- Vertical Delivery Head/Account Delivery Manager responsible for overall governance

## Reverse Transition — Key Points
1. Define and agree reverse transition process during SoW stage
2. Avoid over-commitments
3. Identify key resources for reverse transition
4. Sign-off transition plan and acceptance criteria
5. Customer sign-off on completion and feedback
6. Duly terminated Contract/SoW
7. Document good practices and learning with final Service Report
8. Formal closure in the system (OWBS, SAP/CPro)
